                          Steve Naidamast • 2 days ago

                          I cannot agree with the case for Agile in the ways I have seen it implemented; in most cases it was merely used as a cover for incompetent technical management.

                          Agile is primarily based upon the original XP paradigm, which turned out to be a complete failure if you know the history as to how this nonsense came about. Agile has been an attempt to mitigate the failures of its predecessor. According to most recent project failure statistics, the claim of Agile as a beneficial paradigm has not been born out.

                          Agile is also nothing new even when it was new. It is primarily a re-invention of the software engineering paradigms of 'evolutionary" and\or "incremental" design. However, there are actually 13 software engineering design paradigms, ALL of which have some form of "Waterfall" process involved. In other words, each design paradigm has its own form of preparatory design standard no matter how small the component being developed.

                          The idea that anyone can design good software without a design plan has only been successful as a promotional strategy for people who like to promote the idea of their version of "Rapid Application Development" for software projects that most often break most engineering principals of which software development is a sister category. In reality it simply cannot be done.

                          Software development is the process of building a product, though it is made up of magnetically stored bits and bytes. In essence (and it has been documented as such), it follows the same paradigm as does technical manufacturing, which has become a highly mature and sophisticated process over the years because of that area's attention to quality design and implementation; not the fact that design was mitigated as much as possible.

                          Software Engineering, though in of itself a mature, successful process has not been incorporated nearly to the extent that similar, modernized, engineering principals have been implemented into manufacturing processes leaving software development as immature a process as it was in the 1960s.

                          If this were not the case and Agile was such a panacea maybe then we should deign our cars this way or our aircraft (Well the F-35 appears to have used the Agile concept since the result has been so disastrous). Or maybe we should design life and death systems in this fashion. Who needs design plans for such things as new cardiac surgery processes that are governed by software?

                          If anyone here wants to fly on a plane that was designed and built with an Agile-like process, raise your hands! Aircraft design and its corresponding software development DOES NOT use Agile since they leave that for stupid business people.

                          Finally, the idea that Agile provides for building smaller components to an overall project faster is somewhat disingenuous. Many of the components that senior developers build during a project do not in fact require up front design as our experience has shown what works and what doesn't. Nonetheless, it still takes time to put project specific coding together successfully and the necessary timelines still have to be part of a design plan.

                          All this being said, there are efficient ways to mitigate some of the design aspects to any project since foundational components most often have been implemented in the same ways for many projects and those experienced with them, as I have just noted, do not require extensive design and planning for them. However, the design phase of any project is not just about the technical features but also include risk factor analysis and proper timeline analysis.

                          And it is these two last factors that Agile promoters try so hard to avoid, streamline, or water down so they can look good to their own management, which is of primary importance to them.

                          If such project managers would spend more time teaching business management about the costs and risks of rushing and\or speeding up development processes than maybe we would not be having this continuous debate as to whether Agile is good or not. The fact that we are does not bode well for the Agile world.

                          This is where the actual problem lays with this entire discussion; it is not about software design and development but how fast can a project get done. And that has always been the problem with technical management since most technical management never had a good grasp of software engineering in the first place...

                          Steve Naidamast
                          Sr. Software Engineer

                              Kirill Marchuk Steve Naidamast • 2 days ago

                              OK, then. Look, in a lot cases the Customer does not even understand what he really wants. He has some loosely related "features" in mind, but how is the project getting there, are these features non-contradictory, or even implementable, - he has no idea at all. Working in Waterfall paradigm in this case means that all of these "wishes" be fully analyzed in all their complexity (which is often impossible without creating some prototypes), then the UI/UX prototypes be drawn and agreed upon, only then developers can possibly start coding, and only afterwards the testers will be testing the project against initial requirements, and only after that the deployment begins. And far too often at this point the Customer understands, that in fact he wanted something completely different, or the way these features are implemented, are in fact not necessary, or his marketing position/ambitions are already changed, etc., etc.

                              Agile is a protective measure in this case. It allows to quickly build proof-of-concept versions, allows to adapt to requirement changes more effectively, and most important it bridges the gap between "wanting" and "getting" thus reducing risks of potentially building a huge, nice and excellently working... piece of crap.

                              It's absolutely incorrect to compare the process of creating software for startups (or any consumer business, for that matter) with building space stations, jet fighters, or even houses. Your requirements to space station, MRT-machine or jet fighter, even a house, are a result of decades of expertise, probably paid by thousands of lives, and you are not going to change them every now and then, which happens alas far too often in customer software development.

                              To put it shortly: I would be happy to work in Waterfall paradigm, but it's too big a luxury to afford in real world.

                                                        Samwise Galenorn • 3 days ago

                                                        Nobody talks about how Agile gets in the way of real work.
                                                        I've been through countless Agile oriented software development projects, and it gets in the way.
                                                        Before the Agile oriented project even begins, EVERYTHING is mapped out. This includes expectations of each development sprint, and when they are expected to be completed. But as any real dev/tester/manager knows, stuff happens, and the deadlines are not met.

                                                        Rather than doing real work, the Agile project managers mandate absolute deadlines for the shorter dev periods with no flexibility. This causes the devs and testers to code specifically to the letter of the expectations of the Agile deadlines. The result is a software project riddled with bugs and holes because of unanticipated items that came up in the middle of a software dev cycle.
                                                        A better solution is to try to anticipate for unexpected items. If something comes up, then either address it right then, or add on another cycle somewhere down the line.
                                                        I've been with too many incompetent software managers who stuck to a schedule, and ended up with an inferior product.

                                                        Agile is a load of crap.
