Problem 7.1, Schach page 212

Determine the cohesion of the following methods:

  • editProfitAndTaxRecord
  • editProfitRecordAndTaxRecord
  • readDeliveryRecordAndCheckSalaryPayments
  • computeTheOptimalCostUsingAksen'sAlgorithm
  • measureVaporPressureAndSoundAlarmIfNecessary

Book Solutions:

  • editProfitAndTaxRecord — Functional cohesion
  • editProfitRecordAndTaxRecord — Procedural cohesion
  • readDeliveryRecordAndCheckSalaryPayments — Coincidental cohesion
  • computeTheOptimalCostUsingAksen'sAlgorithm — Functional cohesion
  • measureVaporPressureAndSoundAlarmIfNecessary — Procedural cohesion

Instructor Solutions/Comments:

  • editProfitAndTaxRecord — This module probably has functional cohesion, but a case could be made for informational cohesion, due to the ambiguity of the English name.
  • editProfitRecordAndTaxRecord — This module is definitely performing at least two separate operations, based on the name. No ambiguity here, but one might need to know the operational order to properly label it as procedural. Otherwise, it might be a case of coincidental cohesion.
  • readDeliveryRecordAndCheckSalaryPayments — This one is fairly obvious. . .
  • computeTheOptimalCostUsingAksen'sAlgorithm — From the name of this module, there may not be more than one function; in this case, it may not even HAVE cohesion!
  • measureVaporPressureAndSoundAlarmIfNecessary — Definitely procedural, since the function to sound the alarm follows the check of the vapor pressure.

This Use Case actually follows the prescribed UML format.

Brief Description:
When a couple applies for a mortgage, the Apply for an MSG Mortgage use case enables an MSG staff member to determine whether the mortgage can be granted.
Step-by-Step Description:

Problem 10.11, Schach page 328

You have just joined Angel & Iguassu Software as a software manager. Angel & Iguassu has been developing accounting software for small businesses for many years using the waterfall model, usually with some success. On the basis of your experience, you think that the Unified Process is a far superior way of developing software. Write a report addressed to the vice–president for software development explaining why you believe the organization should switch to the Unified Process. Remember that vice–presidents do not like reports that are more than half a page in length.


Book Solution:

Key points to make include: The fact that the waterfall model has been used "usually with some success" does not necessarily mean that the client's real needs are always met. Customer satisfaction should be the driving force behind all software development, and this can be enhanced by using the Unified Process.


Instructor Solution/Comments:

There are several other key points to make, based on the weaknesses of the waterfall model:

  • Real projects don't seem to follow the sequential flow; changes can cause confusion
  • Often difficult for the customer to state all requirements explicitly at the start of the project
  • The process has difficulty adapting to later requirements changes which are due to any project's natural uncertainty
  • The customer must be patient since a working version of the program won't be delivered until near the end of the development
  • A major design blunder can be disastrous; it will be expensive to fix, and can cause extensive delays.

Problem 5.10, Schach page 143

You are the manager in charge of the software that controls the navigation system for a midget submarine. Three different user–reported faults have to be fixed, and you assign one each to Paul, Quentin, and Rachel. A day later you learn that, to implement each of the three fixes, the same four artifacts must be changed. However, your configuration–control tool is inoperative, so you will have to manage the changes yourself. How will you do it?


Book Solution:

Paul, Quentin, and Rachel make local copies of each of the four modules, and determine what changes each will made. Each in turn then freezes the current version of all four modules, makes changes, and has the changes tested by the SQA group. The changed version becomes the baseline version, local copies of which are then distributed to the remaining maintenance programmers. The baseline versions of the four modules must be under the personal control of the manager to ensure that this procedure is followed exactly in the absence of an automated tool.

Instructor Solution/Comments:

I agree with this in principle; however, there are more issues here which need consideration.

  • First, managers usually don't participate in the coding and maintenance tasks of a project; they are typically far too busy tracking everyone's performance to actually participate in the work. In this situation, one of the programmers would be a more likely candidate for the CM management task.
  • Second, having three people manage changes to four modules is not necessarily a fair division of labor. Unless there are acknowledged differences in programming skill, the person who gets assigned two modules may feel negative impact.
  • Third, having all four modules "frozen" might end up requiring that the changes are made sequentially, rather than in parallel. The book solution suggests that the new modules become the baseline, but in what order? How does the project maintain the idea of only checking in working code? How can the other programmers, for example, be sure they are building their unit tests using Quentin's latest working version, or that it is a completely tested version?
  • Finally, even if the book solution is followed, with the CM system inoperative, how will any merging of files be accomplished? Merging by hand is a very time–consuming and labor–intensive process. Will the project schedule (or the schedule for this bug fix) support the increased time required for this manual operation?

Problem 11.1, Schach page 387

Why should the following constraints not appear in a specification document:

  1. The product must reduce significantly transportation expenses that arise from distributing our beer in central Queensland
  2. The credit card database must be set up at a reasonable cost


Book Solution:

Both constraints superficially appear to be precise and scientific, but neither can be measured or tested. Specifically,

  • (i) The term "significantly" is imprecise — significant for whom: client or developer? Also, this is a reason for developing the product, not a constraint.
  • (ii) The term "reasonable" is imprecise — reasonable for whom: client or developer?

Instructor Solution/Comments:

I agree, but there is more that can be disambiguated (GOD, I love using that word):

The phrase "transportation expenses" is too non–specific for use in a requirements constraint statement. There are quite a few possible expenses associated with transportation, such as gas, truck leasing, maintenance, driver wages, and so on. It is not clear from this constraint whether the total is to be reduced, or if some particular part of the contributing factors needs to be made the focus. Also, the location of "central Queensland" could be made more specific. In the second statement, what about maintaining the credit card database, after it has been "set up at a reasonable cost"?

Problem 6.4, Schach page 169

You have been testing a code artifact for 11 days and found two faults. What does this tell you about the existence of other faults?


Book Solution:

Assuming that good testing techniques have been used, it is unlikely that there are any further faults.

Instructor Solution/Comments:

Well, that's short and sweet. Also, it's not really 100% true. As Edsger W. Dijkstra said in his ACM Turing lecture in 1972:


Today a usual technique is to make a program and then to test it. But: program testing can be a very effective way to show the presence of bugs, but is hopelessly inadequate for showing their absence. The only effective way to raise the confidence level of a program significantly is to give a convincing proof of its correctness. But one should not first make the program and then prove its correctness, because then the requirement of providing the proof would only increase the poor programmer's burden. On the contrary: the programmer should let correctness proof and program grow hand in hand.

What this implies is, no amount of testing, no matter how long you run it, is really sufficient to prove that no other bugs exist in the program. The more complex the application, the more this fact is true. Thorough testing will help, because bugs will be caught, but there is no way to prove 100% that no bugs exist. Even formal mathematical proof techniques may not be enough in all cases.

Of course, this interpretation may also depend on the strength of your definition of the word "unlikely" given in the answer — that's a "weasel word". If your connotation is "possible but not probable" then the book's answer may be considered correct. However, if your connotation leans more toward "highly improbable" then I'm more inclined to disagree with the author.




Problem 6.5, Schach page 169

What are the similarities between a walkthrough and an inspection? What are the differences?


Book Solution:

Both are review processes performed by teams with the aim of finding faults. In both cases, the material is studied by individual team members, and then the material is reviewed by the team as a group.

The major differences are:

  • an inspection is a formal five-step process; a walk-through has two informal steps
  • previously acquired fault statistics play an important role in the inspection process
  • there is a formal component of the inspection process for ensuring that all faults noted are later corrected
  • if more than a certain fraction of the material is changed then it must be submitted for reinspection.

Instructor Solution/Comments:

Actually, the ideas presented in the book, while not incorrect, are not entirely complete either. As we've seen from our class experience, a walk-through has many more than two steps, and is, in fact, a very formalized process. Although there can be informal versions of code walk-throughs, even the "informal" process is still dictated by specific steps. Further, an inspection can have many more than five steps. Indeed, the third bullet above also applies to the formal walk-through process.

Narrative Open the file of mortgages and read each element in turn. For each mortgage, compute the weekly capital repayment, interest payment, and escrow payment for this mortgage. The formulae are as follows:

capital repayment = price / number of mortgage payments

First, determine the weekly mortgage payment on the assumption that the couple can pay the mortgage in full, without a grant. The full weekly mortgage payment is the sum of the capital repayment, interest payment, and escrow payment.

Now determine how much the couple can actually pay, namely, their current weekly income times the maximum permitted income percentage.

If the amount they can actually pay is less than the full amount, they need a grant for the difference.

If a grant needed, add the grant amount to the running total of grants.

Close the file of mortgages.

Return the total expected net payments for the week (the expected total weekly mortgages less the total weekly grants).
