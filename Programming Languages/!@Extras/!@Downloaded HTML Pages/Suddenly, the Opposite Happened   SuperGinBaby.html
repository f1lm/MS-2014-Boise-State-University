Suddenly, the Opposite Happened

October 28, 2014October 28, 2014 | superginbaby

There has been a lot of noise on Twitter, and I suppose in other places, about Haskell users (and functional programmers more generally) being “condescending” and “smug” and thus unwelcoming to newbies. The most recent case I witnessed involved a woman complaining that a conference presentation (I hope I am remembering the details correctly) was condescending because it was titled “Functional Programming is Morally Correct.” As far as I could tell, she was objecting to the title, which is a reference to this paper (and is not necessarily referring to what Americans typically think of as “morally” correct), rather than the content of the presentation. No, I didn’t know the reference of the title either, but it was pointed out in the thread.

The entire thread reminded me of an incident that happened in a sophomore-level literary criticism class while I was in college. We were reading some piece of criticism, and a girl objected that the author had made a reference to Plato that she didn’t understand. The professor pointed out to her that if she did not understand the Plato reference, that was her fault, not the author’s. She objected that he was being condescending. He replied, “You’re an adult now. Take some responsibility for learning.” That actually was a bit condescending; it was also (morally) correct.

“Condescending” and “smug” both imply an attitude by which one party treats the other party as inferior. What the author who referenced Plato and the morally correct authors are doing is the opposite of that. They are making the assumption that their audience is as well-educated and intelligent as themselves, and they are refusing to simplify their writing or fill it with unnecessary verbosity explaining what could be made clear through allusion. They are assuming that if you don’t already know what they mean, you will at least be an adult and take some responsibility for your own learning and find out.

I have come to learning Haskell in an unusual way. I have a master’s degree in linguistics and some background in logic (due to a BA in philosophy). I have no background in either math or computer programming. At all. I was persuaded, somehow, to learn Haskell by a friend with a notable fervor for Haskell advocacy, and because he got me sort of excited about natural language processing, I decided to give it a whirl. He is a person who has been accused in some Twitter brawls of being condescending. But over the course of my Haskell instruction, I have had to ask him questions like, “What is a compiler?” “What are these ‘side effects’ you’re talking about?” “What’s an AST?” “What are these ‘foo’ and ‘bar’ functions?” “OMG WHAT DO I DO, WHAT DO I DO?” (the answer to the last is: Ctrl-C)

A condescending person would have made me feel like an idiot for having to ask for such information. “Oh, dear, if you don’t know what a compiler is by now…*chuckle*” A condescending person would imply that my delicate ladybrain might not be able to understand what a compiler is. The opposite-of-condescending person, like my teacher, assumes, rather, that I have a mundane knowledge gap resulting from the fact that I have no programming background. Everyone in the Haskell community I have talked to has been helpful rather than condescending. They assume I will either understand, figure it out, or ask if I don’t/can’t, whether I ask them or Google. This is the opposite of condescending; this is, in fact, how equals treat each other.

I replied in this particular Twitter thread that I thought charges of condescension frequently revealed more about the complainant than the respondent. Frequently, it was not any remarkable action on the part of the accused that is making the accuser feel inferior; it is the accuser’s own laziness and unwillingness to take responsibility for his/her own learning that is causing the feeling of inferiority. This anti-intellectualism is something I have no respect for. It’s fine if you don’t want to learn something, I suppose, but it’s surely nothing to be proud of.

And finally, at the risk of someone actually reading this and creating some kind of brouhaha, I want to add a final comment: It is often the case that when charges of condescension are lodged against the Haskell community (or the linguistics community, or whatever), they are made by women and allegedly on behalf of women (and/or other minorities) who may be more intimidated by this sort of thing. As a woman, I find this intolerable. Treating you differently in a professional setting *because* you’re a woman is what is condescending; refusing to do so is the opposite of condescending. I do not want men (or women) to dumb their conversations down for me. If I’m interested in learning, I will learn to understand what they’re saying without them doing so. If I’m not, then why do I even care how they talk about the subject? It is deeply offensive to me that women would even lodge this complaint in such terms.

I will say that being a newbie in functional programming, especially with no math or programming experience, can be intimidating. There is *a lot* to learn. The level of abstraction, the math symbols everywhere, the inner workings of the IO monad–yeah, these things can be intimidating. When I talk to Haskellers sometimes, I feel intimidated by the sheer amount I don’t yet know–but not by how they’ve treated me. In my experience, Haskellers are extremely generous about helping the unglamorous noobs. They will sit with you and explain what side-effects are and what compilers do. They will desugar Haskell’s syntax for you. They will list what they wish they had known when they were learning Haskell. They will have lengthy conversations with you about catamorphisms. They freakin love to. They just seem so happy to have new people interested.

And I appreciate that, even when I am overwhelmed.

(The title of this post is a reference to a very fun children’s book called The Opposite. The poor boy in the book is haunted by an Opposite who keeps making the opposite happen whenever the boy tries to do something, but he finally figures out the key to getting rid of it.)

    I think it may be worth digging a bit deeper into the emotional side of the discussion, you kind of skimmed it there.

    I think most often people are having emotional reactions to something because it reminds them of something else from their past. People can come up with first impressions very quickly, even based on a bit of written text. Once they’ve decided “who you are” they can interpret your comments as smug, friendly, or whatever they want. It’s all based on assumptions. I think it’s best not to take these things personally … nobody really know anyone else and they’re reacting to something in their past not to the actual event.

    If someone accuses you or anyone else of being smug on condescending it may be you remind them of someone else who was that way in the past.

    I expect the reason people can experience a community (like the Haskell one) very differently is in no small part because of their past experiences of people and communities, and what kinds of words, phrases, or communication styles trigger them.
