                                            803861337 whitesquall • a day ago

                                            You may want to read up on your history. His grandfather (http://plato.stanford.edu/entr... pioneered boolean algebra, by which we can transform the manipulation of electrical signals into meaningful logic. If that isn't fundamental to computing, I don't know what is. So it doesn't matter that "everybody and their grandmother has claimed that." When you and your grandmother claim it, it isn't true. When he claims it, it is.

                                            I love your comment on how humans and computers become further apart the closer together they are. Take a set with infinitely many members A and a set with infinitely many members B, where A and B are not the same set. Furthermore, there is more than one member of A which is not in B, and more than one member of B which is not in A. Now let's create a set C. We create it by taking A, and changing one of its members which is not in B to be one of the members of B which is not in A. We would then be able to say that C is nearer to being equal to B than A, since it has one more member which is shared with B than A does.

                                            So no, being able to split things into infinitely many parts does not mean that things become less similar to each other the more similar to each other they become. That would be stupid.

                                                                            pvoosen zviboger • 7 hours ago

                                                                            It is absolutely the case that Paul Werbos invented a version of backprop more than a decade before the PDP group published, though in a different context: as a method "for estimating a dynamic model to predict nationalism and social communications," as Werbos later put it.

                                                                            Others also separately invented their own version of backprop concurrently or just before PDP: David Parker and, notably, Yann LeCun. As LeCun also pointed out to me, optimal control theory had used algorithms very close to backprop since the 1960s. Good inventions rarely come once.

                                                                            I wish I could have included this context in the story, but not everything makes it in, unfortunately. Others I would have loved to mention: Andrew Ng, now at Baidu, so influential in getting Google to adopt DL and generous in talking with me; John Hopfield, who galvanized so many physicists to investigate neural nets in the 1980s; Kunihiko Fukushima's work on the cognitron; Hubel and Wiesel’s work on the visual cortex, and its importance to Fukushima and LeCun's convolutional neural nets; and the many grad students and postdocs in Hinton's lab and others who have contributed to the third resurgence.
