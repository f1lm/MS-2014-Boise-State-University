Course Description:

COMPSCI 557 Artificial Intelligence.  This course will introduce and study several core learning algorithms in the field of artificial intelligence and machine learning.  Coverage includes version space learning, decision trees, artificial neural networks, Bayesian learning techniques, instance based learning, and genetic algorithms. 

We will present a broad introduction to several of the more-popular inductive learning algorithms used in the field of Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning. Class lectures will discuss general issues in machine learning and artificial intelligence, as well as present established algorithms.

Required Text:

Machine Learning by Tom Mitchell, McGraw-Hill.  The order of reading is on the schedule and the assigned chapters must be read before the class lecture. Note that the schedule is only tentative.  The lecture will cover topics from the reading where additional help, emphasis, and extensions beyond the text are deemed valuable.  Time will not always allow complete coverage of all aspects of the reading, but you are still expected to read and understand the text.  You will be responsible for all information from the assigned reading, even that which is not explicitly covered in class, except where specifically noted, on examinations. 


In addition to the text, we will also read a few papers taken from scientific journals, as well as other supplementary material.  These will be handed out in class. 

 

Class Objectives:

After taking this course the student will:

 

  • Be able to compare and contrast the various approaches to learning, their strengths and weaknesses and so forth.

  • Be able to read, understand and critique scientific articles in AI and Machine Learning.

  • Be given the background and understanding necessary to be able to propose original research in the field of Artificial Intelligence and inductive learning algorithms in particular with potential follow-up in a graduate research program.

  • Learn research skills applicable to other fields of computer science.

  • Know how to use different learning algorithms to solve problems (hands on experience).

  • Each student will be given an opportunity to hone their presentation and technical writing skills.

Annotated Bibliography/Survey: 

You are to create an annotated bibliography (or survey) on 15 to 20 scientific papers from academic journals in the area of AI and machine learning algorithms.  The papers in your bibliography should be ones that you looked up yourself, and not ones that I present or hand out in class.  You should try to find papers that are interesting.   Papers that present novel learning algorithms, or that attempt to improve some learning algorithm are preferable to papers that just use some learning algorithm to solve a particular problem â€“ unless you would like to focus on a particular learning problem, such as character recognition, and you want to review some of the approaches which have been taken.  Your bibliography should contain at least 5 papers that were published in this century (year 2000 forwards).  You should summarize each paper, and I would also like to see you critique each paper, weaknesses and strengths of their approach, and so forth.   You may wish to choose a focused topic/area to look at (for example, only review papers on decision trees, or papers on document region identification) or you may not want to do that.  Itâ€™s up to you.  Write an introductory section (a few paragraphs) stating what your survey is going to cover, why you chose to do that, what you were hoping to discover, where your interests lie, etc.  I also want to see a conclusion â€“ write a few paragraphs stating what youâ€™ve learned, summarize discoveries â€“ for example, if you reviewed OCR approaches state what you think is the best approach(es).  Talk about which papers you liked the most and why.  Maybe even suggest where future work/research in the area could go, possible improvements, etc.  The final draft of this will be due towards the end of the semester, but I will ask for a preliminary bibliography towards the middle of the semester that should have at least 8 papers in it, so get cracking.

 

Mini Papers/Projects/HW:

You will be assigned a few projects throughout the semester to work on. For example, I may ask that you experiment with some of the different learning algorithms â€“ decision trees, neural networks, instance based learning, Bayesian approaches, etc.,  perhaps asking you to test these algorithms on a few different learning problems and report results.  Each project will typically require a ~3-5 page write-up.  These will be due throughout the semester.

 

Final Project:

An in-depth effort on a particular aspect of machine learning. A literature search in the area is expected with your paper referencing what you find. Good projects are typically as follows: Best: Some of your own original thinking and proposal of a learning model, paradigm, system, etc. This (and other projects) are typically well benefited by some computer simulation to bear out potential. Very Good: Starting from an in-depth study of some current model, strive to extend it through some new mechanisms. Not Bad: A study of a current model with an in-depth analysis of its strengths, weaknesses, potential, and suggested research. Not Good: A description of a current model. The earlier you start the better. Note that in a semester course like this, you will have to choose a topic when we have only covered half of the material. That does not mean your project must cover items related to the first half of the semester. You should use your own initiative and the resources available (library literature, texts, me, etc.) to peruse and find any topic of interest to you, regardless of whether we have or will cover it in class. There are many interesting things that we will not have time to cover.

 

You are to turn in a paper detailing your efforts.  This paper should be in the format of a journal article.  Use the style specified by http://computer.org/author/style/cs-style.htm .  The paper should be between 8 and 15 pages and is due on the last day of class.
