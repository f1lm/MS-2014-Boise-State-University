    "AS AIS DEVELOP, WE MIGHT HAVE TO ENGINEER WAYS TO PREVENT CONSCIOUSNESS IN THEM"

    Well, that's easy, just keep going the way we are now.

    First, consciousness isn't emergent in a closed computing system. There are strict rules, and the computer becomes worthless if it can't follow those rules- even for computing systems where educated guesses are made.

    Second, AI development is not the same as evolution where multitudes of emergent consciousness algorithms are thrown against a wall to see what just sits there and what becomes conscious- even if you you used a computer like Watson to help you design those algorithms.

    Third, consciousness is a spectrum. It is not a yes/no function where one day the computer is a piece of hardware and software and the next day it's a living being. I've got a fish. It's self aware. It's conscious. It thinks very little about the world around it and it's place in the world. I also have a toddler. She shares a lot with the fish in terms of her world view, but she does have a sense of the future, the past, and causality.

    Fourth, a mind is different than a brain. A mind exists to make sense of inputs from the world around it. By definition, it needs a "body" of some sort that interacts and learns from the world around it. A conscious artificial mind with no body (and no rights) would be a nightmare scenario for any human. Also, the question would arise of whether that brain continued to constitute a mind. What would it think about with no inputs? How would it even learn to be a mind?

    Creating very complicated AI brains that solve problems (and create new ones) is vastly different than creating a self-aware mind that thinks for itself with willfulness and intent. The former is useful. The latter would be driven by its own selfish interests, and would make for a poor computer. How would you even motivate it other than enforced servitude and threats to turn it off or disconnect parts of it?

    In other words, creating a conscious computer doesn't make sense whatsoever if you want useful work out of it. Creating AIs that solve problems and work well, but aren't driven by their own motivations and desires is far more preferable.

                                              umadbro Patrick O'brien a month ago

                                              Okay. Again: I'm not talking about Watson specifically (while most of the article is). One quick note beforehand: As a programmer, you *should* know that you can't even know enough about your program to decide if it halts or not ;-) (unless it's a really naive and simplistic program).

                                              To get an idea about the sort of program I'm talking about (instead of Watson) have a look at the Human Brain Project (although they massively over-sell their pitch).

                                              Consider the following levels of abstraction: 1.) A (computational) model of the human brain down to atomic level, modelling interactions using our best representation of quantum chemistry. 2.) A neural network built to exactly mimic the map of the human brain, down to each and every single neuron and synapse. 3.) A top-down approach like Watson.

                                              Clearly option 1 is "computationally expensive", to an extent that it will probably never be feasible. (Currently here are coarse-grained simulations of bacterial cells using classical and not quantum mechanics). However if we "get it right", i.e. put in all parts, and describe all rules properly, in principle this constitutes a rigorous simulation of an actual brain. It might not be useful because it's too slow .. as I wrote in one of my earlier comments, we are "emulating" reality, and we will lose several orders of magnitude in speed. I doubt we could have a conversation with this artificial brain.
                                              Option 2 doesn't care about atoms but just about connections. Interactions between neurons are a bit more of a black box than in the atomistic simulation, so it will be difficult "to get it right" and use a lot of fitting against *real* biological data. However this is compuationally much less demanding than option 1.
                                              Option 3 doesn't specifically aim to capture the biology of the brain .. it doesn't care about the number of synapses, actual biological circuits in the brain etc. However it is feasible with today's computational power.

                                              I think in order to have the chance "to see consciousness emerge", and ultimately understand our own consciousness, and what makes us human, better, we need to aim somewhere between option 1 and 2. The question is how much detail will be enough .. hopefully closer to option 2 than 1, because I'm keen to see it happen in my lifetime :)

                                                                                        paulhummerman umadbro a month ago

                                                                                        There is a rigorous sense in which emergence is indeed the sum of the (interacting) parts. Take the simplest and most familiar example of emergence: the solidity of ice. A single isolated water molecule cannot be said to be either solid or liquid, regardless of temperature. A large number of noninteracting water molecules (a gas) is neither solid not liquid. But a large number (>100) of interacting molecules can be either a solid or liquid, at least to a very close approximation. Of course the interaction, whilst only with neighbors, is not linear (which is why there can be interesting emergent properties). The nonlinear interaction between neurons is only weakly constrained by proximity, so the brain has many interesting self-organizing states.
                                                                                        Fast forward to consciousness, though I prefer to focus on to the closely-related "understanding", perhaps the most interesting property of the brain. (Consciouness itself is less interesting, since it largely corresponds to wakefulness, a well-defined and largely understood physiological process (brain stem activation etc). I think that the point of the article is that recent advances in AI suggest that some forms of mechanical understanding are round the corner. Whether there are additional "emergences" (barriers to high level of AI, eg via deep learning) that need to be understood is an interesting open question. I suspect that we will be able to identify such barriers, but that we will not be able to surpass them in hardware. We may be stuck with the brains we've got, but I agree with the article that machine learning/AI could provide our brains with valuable additional props (like speech, writing, printing etc)

                                                                                                        Meeeee claynaff a month ago

                                                                                                        What you are describing is a perfect example of open-loop architecture. Or "clock-winder" programming. The programmer sets the initial rules, and the simulation takes over from there.

                                                                                                        That sort of programming is useful for running scenarios, but it (likely) makes a poor tool for calculating or problem solving where a discrete answer is sought.

                                                                                                        Again, though, this takes us back to why you'd try to create a cognizant intelligence in that manner, and what sort of body and civil rights would it need. The only reason to do it is because you want to parent a machine-based "being." There aren't any problems it could solve (that we could conceptualize) that we can't solve with non-cognizant computers, but it would have all the ethical, practical, and logistical problems self-motivation brings to the table. What happens if it refuses to work?

                                                                                                        Additionally, that sort of "being" would need to be nurtured, or it would just sit there. With open-loop architecture you couldn't just make a copy of the starting state program, push "run" and have it emerge as a duplicate of another being. You'd have to raise it and provide it with sensors to interact with the outside world (or a virtual world). The more different the AI's upbringing, the harder it will be for humans to relate to it (just like with people).

                                                                                                        Additionally, you'd have to provide it with some sort of reason or impetus for cognizant thinking. A fish survives perfectly well because its body provides enough functional interaction to perpetuate it. A human has a different body, and different adaptations in order to survive. One of those adaptations is cognizance, but human cognizance is developed through interactions with our world and our experiences- we don't get it right out of the box. Perhaps an AI being could have it's neural matrix and memories completely copied over to create a clone of itself, but the moment it awoke, it would start to differ from it's twin- just like human twins. This doesn't necessarily create a problem if you want to create artificial beings, but it's terrible if you want to create a product line.
